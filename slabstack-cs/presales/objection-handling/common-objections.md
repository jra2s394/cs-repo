# Common Objections

Real objections from prospect calls and how to respond. Update this file when you hear a new objection more than twice.

---

## "We already have a system for this"

**What they usually mean:** They have a spreadsheet, a dispatch tool, and email — and they're not sure what would change.

**Response:**
"Most of our customers had something in place before Slabstack — usually some combination of spreadsheets, dispatch, and email. What changes isn't the existence of a system, it's the time spent moving information between systems. The question we ask is: how much time per week does your team spend on double entry? When the answer is more than a few hours, Slabstack pays for itself."

**Next step:** Ask them to quantify the time and rerun the ROI math with their number.

---

## "It's too expensive"

**What they usually mean:** Either the budget genuinely isn't there, or they haven't connected the price to a return.

**Response:**
"Price is fair to push on. Let's back into it from value. You told me a billing error costs you about $X and you have about Y per year. Slabstack pricing comes out to roughly Z per month — so if it prevents one error a quarter, it's already paid for. Does that math line up with your numbers?"

**Next step:** Walk through the ROI calculator together. Don't drop price.

---

## "We need to integrate with [dispatch system]"

**What they usually mean:** They want confirmation it'll work with what they already use.

**Response:**
"Good question — let me confirm whether we already have that integration. If we do, I can show you exactly what syncs. If we don't, I'll be straight with you: we'd want to scope it before you sign, so you know the timeline before committing."

**Next step:** Check the integrations list. Loop in CS Eng if needed.

---

## "Send me information and I'll review it"

**What they usually mean:** They're either not the decision-maker, or they're trying to end the call politely.

**Response:**
"Happy to. To make sure I send the right thing — is the next step you reviewing it alone, or sharing with a team? If it's a team, I'd love to be on that call too so I can answer the questions that come up. What does that timeline look like?"

**Next step:** Get a date on the calendar before the call ends. A "send info" with no follow-up booked is a dead deal.

---

## "We're not ready to make a change right now"

**What they usually mean:** Either timing genuinely isn't right (busy season, hiring, etc.) or they don't yet feel the pain.

**Response:**
"That's fair. When you say 'not ready' — is it more that the timing is wrong, or that the pain isn't sharp enough yet to justify the work of switching? Those are two different problems and I'd want to handle them differently."

**Next step:**
- If timing: agree on a specific re-engagement date and put it on the calendar.
- If pain: revisit the discovery questions. Something was missed.

---

## "I need to talk to my partner / accountant / dispatcher"

**What they usually mean:** They're not the sole decision-maker, and they should have said so earlier.

**Response:**
"Of course — those folks should weigh in. Would it work to get a 20-minute call on the books with them this week? I can run a focused walkthrough on the parts they care about. That's faster than you having to retell what I showed you today."

**Next step:** Schedule the multi-stakeholder call before this call ends.

## How to Use This File

- Don't memorize. Read it once a quarter so the patterns stay fresh.
- When a new objection comes up that isn't here, add it within a week.
- Pair this with `competitive-positioning.md` when the objection is comparison-based.
